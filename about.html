Introduction

I was born August 24, 1997 in Vallejo, California. I’ve lived in various locations around the state for my entire life, but I have been a Bay Area local since 2010. I am going to graduate from CSU East Bay in Hayward at the end of 2024 with my Bachelor of Fine Arts in Interaction and Game Design. My interests have always been of a technical nature. My hobbies involving video gaming and computer hardware have developed into a vested interest in software development and user experience design, and how said user experience can affect the way people think or act.

On top of a genuine love for games and interactivity, I use my technical knowledge and skills in HTML/CSS, JavaScript, UX/UI Design, Adobe Suite, Unreal Engine, and other programs to complete any project I set out for. I enjoy working on things that challenge me to either hone my prior skills or learn something new. I am committed to creating the best possible user experiences down to the smallest details.

Early Life & Hobbies

From a young age, I have always loved to learn thanks to my parents. They taught me to appreciate and ask questions about the world around me, which translates into the unquenchable desire to create meaningful experiences I have today. Aside from video games, I enjoy science and nature as well as the exploration of other cultures. I love going to Lake Tahoe because it is a treasured family vacation spot as well as a place that is very close to nature. I also enjoy building and painting model kits in my free time, as it allows me to be creative and hands-on while also having a social aspect of sharing with friends. I also enjoy watching my favorite shows and movies. Some of my favorite movies are The Lord of the Rings, Star Wars, No Country for Old Men, Goodfellas, and Shrek among many others. My favorite shows include Dragon Ball, Star Trek: The Next Generation, The Sopranos, Parks and Recreation, 30 Rock, and more. I also enjoy spending time with my friends and engaging in discussions about each of our favorite pastimes.

Video Games & Me

From the day I was old enough to pick up a controller, video games have held a special place in my heart. There is something undeniably profound and deeply satisfying about immersing yourself in a world with a unique set of rules and goals. That is why I have dedicated my life to the study of everything games; their history, their design, and the countless diverse communities surrounding them. More specifically, I am interested in creating games and levels that provide an experience that is immersive, engaging, and fun.

Among all my hobbies, video games have always been my choice form of escapism. I had my share of struggles growing up, but no matter how stressful things got, I could always be whisked away to my favorite worlds and experience stories and levels from games such as Pokemon, Mario, Jak and Daxter, Ratchet and Clank, World of Warcraft, Portal, and too many more to possibly list. Moreover, gaming cooperatively with friends in games like Terraria, Minecraft, Castle Crashers, Counter Strike, Team Fortress 2, and many more was a highly formative bonding experience for my friends and I. Although the struggles of adulthood are all too real for me today, I still find much comfort in games both old and new, and diving deeper into the development and design of my favorite games has become a passion of mine.

My first console was a Nintendo 64 that my dad got for my brother and I, when I was about 4 or 5. We have many fond memories of games like Pokemon Stadium, Super Mario 64, Donkey Kong 64, Tony Hawk’s Pro Skater 2, Goldeneye, and Turok. I distinctly remember a sense of wonder, especially with regards to Mario 64; like many gamers, I was enthralled by the three-dimensional world and fluid gameplay. Donkey Kong 64 was also very formative for me; the vibrant world and goofy characters drew me in, and it felt like there were endless secrets to discover in the game world. Although I never beat it due to its excessive length, it helped instill a love for exploration in virtual worlds that I still hold today. I was enticed to explore more video games and see what else among endless possibilities awaited me.

After the Nintendo 64, I experienced many old classics such as Pac-Man, Pong, Mappy, Dig Dug, Pitfall, and more via plug-and-play consoles. I did not realize the importance of those games back then, but I now appreciate them for the impact they had and the simple elegance of their design in spite of technical limitations. The next console my brother and I owned was a PlayStation 2. My biggest sinks on the console were Jak and Daxter, Sly Cooper, and Ratchet and Clank. Jak and Daxter in particular was hugely influential to me; it was the first time I experienced a cinematic story in a video game. I found myself in a vibrant, persistent world that felt lived in and was bursting with colorful characters, on an epic quest across the land to save my friend. The expressive cutscenes, the variety in levels, and the fluidity of Jak’s movement all coalesced into a highly immersive and unique experience for the time. When I got my hands on Jak 2 and 3, I was further engrossed by the story of time travel, monsters, and killer robots, and I thoroughly enjoyed the new gameplay elements that got added as the trilogy progressed. However, the original holds a special place in my heart as it laid the groundwork for what I would consider to be one of the best trilogies 3D platformers of all time. It would go on to inspire my own game, Lucky and the Sunken Castle. Ratchet and Clank and Sly Cooper are also important to me; I’ll never forget the countless waves of enemies I would have to face with Ratchet’s cartoonish arsenal. Conversely, playing Sly Cooper was when I realized that action games don’t have to entirely revolve around combat. The stealth gameplay paired with the explorable levels was intoxicating.

The first time I owned my own video game was Pokemon Ruby Version on the GameBoy Advance. Although I am highly critical of Pokemon as a video game franchise today, it stems from a love that began on the GBA. It is a franchise I hold incredibly dear to my heart, as I would go on to play every main series Pokemon game to be released thereafter. I cannot begin to count the number of adventures I have had across all of them; of all the games I played growing up, Pokemon as a franchise is the one I spent the most time in. Its wondrous world taught me to be myself and find joy in the mundane, and it was my first time playing a game that involved more strategy versus reflexes. Every so often, I still find enjoyment in going back to those older games and trying them again with an all new team. Nostalgia aside, I believe the teambuilding and strategic gameplay of Pokemon was a triumph in both art direction and game design for all the endless variety it offers.

It was around the time when my family got a PlayStation 3 that I began to realize that everything in games was consciously designed, and I started paying attention to the difference between games that I thought were good and bad. What circumstances lead to “bad” games? How do you make a “good” game? Is it something I can do? I still ask myself these broad questions regularly. I played many of the classics from that generation such as Uncharted, The Last of Us, Assassin’s Creed IV, Call of Duty Modern Warfare, and more. Games were becoming more complex just as I was becoming more analytical and intrigued by them. Around this time, I attended ID Tech Camp at Santa Clara University; a summer program for kids to learn how to make simple video games. I made a 2D side scrolling shooter that has unfortunately been lost to time; it was rudimentary, but it was a great experience and my first taste of game development. This is when I really started considering game design and development as something I could pursue for my own career.

As the years went on, I still enjoyed playing on consoles as I was getting into PC gaming; my current custom built PC is still my go-to video game machine today. The family computer slowly became mine as I grinded hours away in World of Warcraft, Terraria, Minecraft, Portal, Team Fortress 2, and many more.
