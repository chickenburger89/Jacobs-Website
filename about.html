Introduction

I was born August 24, 1997 in Vallejo, California. I’ve lived in various locations around the state for my entire life, but I have been a Bay Area local since 2010. I am going to graduate from CSU East Bay in Hayward at the end of 2024 with my Bachelor of Fine Arts in Interaction and Game Design. My interests have always been of a technical nature. My hobbies involving video gaming and computer hardware have developed into a vested interest in software development and user experience design, and how said user experience can affect the way people think or act.

On top of a genuine love for games and interactivity, I use my technical knowledge and skills in HTML/CSS, JavaScript, UX/UI Design, Adobe Suite, Unreal Engine, and other programs to complete any project I set out for. I enjoy working on things that challenge me to either hone my prior skills or learn something new. I am committed to creating the best possible user experiences down to the smallest details.

Early Life & Hobbies

From a young age, I have always loved to learn thanks to my parents. Video Games & Me

From the day I was old enough to pick up a controller, video games have held a special place in my heart. There is something undeniably profound and deeply satisfying about immersing yourself in a world with a unique set of rules and goals. That is why I have a vested interest in the study of everything games; their history, their design, and the countless diverse communities surrounding them. More specifically, I am interested in creating user experiences that are immersive and engaging.

Growing up, I could always be whisked away to my favorite worlds and experience stories and levels from games such as Pokémon, Mario, Jak and Daxter, Ratchet and Clank, World of Warcraft, Portal, and too many more to possibly list. Moreover, gaming cooperatively with friends in games like Terraria, Minecraft, Castle Crashers, Counter Strike, Team Fortress 2, and many more was a highly formative bonding experience for my friends and I. I still find much comfort in games both old and new, and diving deeper into the development and design of my favorite games has become a passion of mine.

My first console was a Nintendo 64 that my dad got for my brother and I, when I was about 4 or 5. We have many fond memories of games like Pokémon Stadium, Super Mario 64, Donkey Kong 64, Tony Hawk’s Pro Skater 2, Goldeneye, and Turok. Like many gamers, I was enthralled by the three-dimensional world and fluid gameplay of games like Mario 64 and DK64. It helped instill a love for exploration in virtual worlds that I still hold today.

After the Nintendo 64, I experienced many old classics such as Pac-Man, Pong, Mappy, Dig Dug, Pitfall, and more via plug-and-play consoles. I appreciate many retro games for the impact they had and the simple elegance of their design in spite of technical limitations. The next console my brother and I owned was a PlayStation 2. My biggest sinks on the console were Jak and Daxter, Sly Cooper, and Ratchet and Clank. Jak and Daxter in particular was the first time I experienced a cinematic story in a video game. I found myself in a vibrant, persistent world that felt lived in and was bursting with colorful characters, on an epic quest across the land to save my friend. The expressive cutscenes, the variety in levels, and the fluidity of Jak’s movement all coalesced into a highly immersive and unique experience for the time. The series would go on to be my own inspiration for our game Lucky and the Sunken Castle.

The first time I owned my own video game was Pokémon Ruby Version on the GameBoy Advance, and I would go on to play every main series Pokémon game to be released thereafter. Its wondrous world taught me to be myself and find joy in the mundane, and it was my first time playing a game that involved more strategy versus reflexes. Every so often, I still find enjoyment in going back to those older games and trying them again with an all new team. Nostalgia aside, I believe the teambuilding and strategic turn-based combat of Pokémon was a triumph in both art direction and game design for all the endless variety it offers.

It was around the time when my family got a PlayStation 3 that I began to notice the conscious design behind everything I was seeing in games, and I started paying attention to the difference between games that I thought were good and bad. What circumstances lead to “bad” games? How do you make a “good” game? Is it something I can do? I still ask myself these broad questions regularly. I played many of the classics from that generation such as Uncharted, The Last of Us, Assassin’s Creed IV, Call of Duty Modern Warfare, Skyrim, and more. Games were becoming more complex just as I was becoming more analytical and intrigued by them. Around this time, I attended ID Tech Camp at Santa Clara University; a summer program for kids to learn how to make simple video games. I made a 2D side scrolling shooter that has unfortunately been lost to time; it was rudimentary, but it was a great experience and my first taste of game development. This is when I really started considering game design and development as something I could pursue for my own career.

The family computer slowly became mine as I grinded hours away in World of Warcraft, Terraria, Minecraft, Portal, Team Fortress 2, and many more. As the years went on, I still enjoyed playing on consoles as I was getting into PC gaming; my current custom build is still my go-to video game machine today.
